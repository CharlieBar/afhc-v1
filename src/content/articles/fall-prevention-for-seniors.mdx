## Why Falls Are a Medical Emergency (Not Just an Accident)

Every 11 seconds, an older adult is treated in an emergency room for a fall injury. Every 19 minutes, an older adult dies from a fall.

In Illinois, falls are the leading cause of both fatal and non-fatal injuries among adults 65 and older. And here's what makes falls uniquely dangerous: they're often predictable and preventable — if you know what to look for.

A fall isn't just bad luck. It's typically the result of a combination of factors: muscle weakness, balance problems, medication side effects, vision changes, and environmental hazards — often in a home that hasn't been updated to match the resident's current physical abilities.

This guide will walk through the home room-by-room and give you the specific changes that make the biggest difference.

## Who Is Most at Risk?

Before the room-by-room assessment, identify whether your loved one has elevated fall risk:

**High-Risk Indicators:**
- One or more falls in the past year
- Fear of falling (often leads to reduced activity, which worsens muscle weakness — a dangerous cycle)
- Difficulty rising from a chair without using arms
- Unsteady gait or shuffling walk
- Medications including blood pressure drugs, sedatives, sleep aids, or diuretics
- Vision problems — particularly depth perception or peripheral vision loss
- Urinary urgency (rushing to the bathroom is a common fall trigger)
- Parkinson's disease, stroke history, or other neurological conditions

If three or more apply, your loved one needs a professional fall risk assessment — not just home modifications. Our physical and occupational therapists perform these assessments as part of home health care. **Call (773) 775-2588** to arrange one.

## Room-by-Room Home Safety Assessment

### Bathroom (Highest Risk Area)

The bathroom is responsible for the largest share of in-home falls. The combination of wet surfaces, lowering and rising movements, and often poor lighting makes it uniquely dangerous.

**Essential Modifications:**
- **Grab bars** in the shower or tub — not suction-cup bars, but professionally installed bars anchored to wall studs. One beside the toilet as well. This is non-negotiable.
- **Non-slip mat** in the tub or shower with good suction
- **Non-slip rug** outside the tub — not a throw rug, but a properly secured bath mat
- **Raised toilet seat** if the standard height makes rising difficult
- **Shower chair or bench** so bathing doesn't require prolonged standing
- **Handheld showerhead** so your loved one can shower seated
- **Night light** — essential for nighttime bathroom trips
- **Remove the threshold** or use a threshold ramp if getting into the tub is difficult

### Bedroom

Most falls happen at night or in the early morning — getting up to use the bathroom in the dark, or rising too quickly from bed.

**Essential Modifications:**
- **Clear path** from bed to bathroom — no furniture, no rugs, no obstacles
- **Motion-activated night light** that turns on automatically when they get up
- **Non-slip rugs only** — secure all area rugs with non-slip backing or remove them
- **Bed height** — the bed should allow feet to touch the floor flat when sitting on the edge
- **Bed rail or grab bar** if rising from bed is difficult
- **Phone within reach** of the bed at all times
- Keep **glasses and any walking aids** right at bedside

### Living Room and Main Areas

**Key Changes:**
- **Secure all area rugs** or remove them — throw rugs are one of the top fall hazards
- **Clear walking paths** — no electrical cords crossing walkways, no footstools in paths
- **Furniture arrangement** — ensure there are surfaces to hold onto between seating and destinations
- **Adequate lighting** — older eyes need more light; add floor lamps and ensure bulbs are high-wattage
- **Phone accessible** from main seating area — so they're never rushing across the room to answer it
- **Remote controls, glasses, medications** within reach of primary chair — reduces unnecessary trips

### Kitchen

**Key Changes:**
- **Frequently used items at accessible heights** — between shoulder and hip level, not requiring reaching above or crouching below
- **Non-slip mat** in front of the sink and stove
- **Step stool with handle** if higher items must be accessed (not regular step stools)
- **Good lighting** — under-cabinet lighting for work surfaces
- **Clear countertops** — clutter increases the chance of reaching and losing balance

### Stairs

If your loved one has stairs, they deserve special attention.

**Essential Changes:**
- **Handrails on both sides** — if there's only one, add a second
- **Secure railings** — test every railing for wobble; a loose railing is a fall waiting to happen
- **Non-slip treads** on each step
- **Light switch at both top and bottom** — never navigating stairs in the dark
- **Consider a stairlift** for anyone who has difficulty with stairs

### Outside and Entryways

Falls don't only happen inside.

**Key Modifications:**
- **Good exterior lighting** — motion-activated lights at all entrances
- **Handrail at front steps** — on the dominant hand side
- **Non-slip porch surfaces** — especially important in Chicago winters
- **Address uneven walkways** — cracked sidewalks and uneven pavers are significant hazards
- **Remove ice and snow promptly** — or arrange for this to be done

## The Role of Physical and Occupational Therapy

Home modifications alone are not enough for many patients. Muscle weakness, balance deficits, and gait abnormalities must be addressed through physical therapy — because a grab bar only helps if a person has enough strength to use it effectively.

Our **physical therapists** build personalized exercise programs to strengthen legs, improve balance, and retrain gait patterns — dramatically reducing fall risk.

Our **occupational therapists** perform comprehensive home safety assessments as part of our home health visits — identifying hazards, recommending specific modifications, and training patients and families on safe movement techniques.

Both services are covered by Medicare for qualifying patients.

## After a Fall: Don't Wait to Get Help

If your loved one has fallen — even if they seem unhurt — it's time to call for a professional assessment. Falls often cause injuries that aren't immediately apparent. And the first fall significantly increases the risk of a second.
